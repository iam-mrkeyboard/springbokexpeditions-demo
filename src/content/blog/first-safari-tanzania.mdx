First safaris are usually a mix of excitement and quiet anxiety. You have a vague picture - the open-top vehicle, the sunset, the leopard on a branch - but you don't really know what the days will feel like. Here's a practical guide to what to expect on a Tanzania safari.

## The shape of a safari day

Most safari days follow a similar rhythm. Here's what a typical day looks like:

**5:30 am** - Wake-up call with tea or coffee brought to your room or tent. The early start is worth it: the animals are most active in the cool of the morning, and the light is best for photography.

**6:00 am** - Game drive. You're in the vehicle with your guide, often in a shared vehicle with other guests, often private. The morning drive usually runs 3 to 4 hours.

**10:00 am** - Back to the lodge for a big breakfast, then a few hours of downtime. The middle of the day is hot and the animals rest, so most lodges encourage a slow morning. Read, swim, write postcards.

**3:30 pm** - High tea, then the afternoon game drive. This is the second-best light of the day, and the animals become active again as the temperature drops.

**6:30 pm** - Sundowners. Most lodges and camps will set up a small bar in a scenic spot - a kopje (rocky outcrop), a riverbank, an open plain. You watch the sunset, drink a gin and tonic, and head back to camp.

**8:00 pm** - Dinner. Most safari lodges serve a three-course dinner, often with a mix of international and local dishes.

**9:30 pm** - In bed. Most people are exhausted by 9 pm and asleep by 10.

Some lodges offer night drives (Tarangire, Lake Manyara) - these use a spotlight and a ranger to find nocturnal animals. Worth doing at least once.

## The game drives

The vehicle is an open-top 4x4 - usually a Toyota Land Cruiser or a Land Rover. The roof pops up so you can stand and look around (this is the iconic safari position). The windows are open, the air is warm, and you're often 30 to 50 feet from the animals.

Your guide is in the front seat, in radio contact with other guides. When a guide sees something interesting, they call it in on the radio - "lion, lion, southern bank of the river." Within minutes, a few vehicles converge.

You'll spend most of the day in the vehicle - 6 to 8 hours total, with breaks. Bring a book, a camera, and a sense of patience. The animals don't perform on demand. Some days you'll see five prides of lions; other days you'll see a few zebra and a lot of grass. This is part of it.

## What you'll see

The northern circuit (Tarangire, Lake Manyara, the Ngorongoro Crater, the Serengeti) has one of the densest wildlife populations on Earth. On a typical 7-day safari, you can reasonably expect to see:

- **Lions:** 80%+ chance. Multiple prides.
- **Elephants:** 90%+ chance. Hundreds in Tarangire alone.
- **Leopards:** 50–70% chance. Harder to spot (they're in trees) but the guides are very good.
- **Cheetahs:** 50–70% chance, especially in the Serengeti.
- **Rhinos:** 40–60% chance, in the Ngorongoro Crater specifically (the only reliable place in East Africa).
- **Hippos, crocodiles, giraffes, zebras, wildebeest, gazelles:** essentially guaranteed.

Bird life is extraordinary - over 1,000 species in Tanzania. Your guide will know the raptors, the rollers, the bee-eaters, the hornbills.

## The lodges

Safari lodges in Tanzania range from rustic tented camps to ultra-luxury. Most first-time safari-goers are surprised by the standard of the mid-range and luxury options. Even the mid-range places are typically:

- Permanent tented suites or solid-walled chalets with en-suite bathrooms
- Swimming pools
- Three meals a day, with beer, wine, and soft drinks included
- Plug points in the rooms (Type G, British-style)
- Hot water (sometimes solar-heated, sometimes not - both work)

The "rustic" tier (budget tented camping) is a different experience - shared bathroom facilities, basic meals, and a more "Out of Africa" feel. We don't usually recommend this for first-timers, but it's a valid choice if you want a more authentic experience.

## Things nobody tells you

**The roads are bumpy.** Safari roads are dirt tracks, often heavily rutted. If you have back problems, mention it to your guide and ask them to drive carefully. Most vehicles have seat belts and a cushion.

**The dust is real.** Especially in the dry season. Don't bring a white shirt. Don't bring a white hat. Leave the fancy camera bag at home. A soft daypack is more practical than a hard case.

**You'll be hot.** Even in the cool season (June–August), the afternoons can be 28–30°C. Drink water constantly. Your guide will keep you supplied, but the temptation is to drink less so you don't need the toilet. Drink the water anyway.

**WiFi is patchy.** Most lodges have WiFi, but it's slow and unreliable. Treat it as a digital detox. If you need to be online, get a local SIM at the airport (Vodacom or Airtel, about $10 for 10GB).

**Tipping matters.** Guides and camp staff rely on tips as a meaningful part of their income. The typical range is $20–25 per day for your guide, $10–15 per day for the camp staff. We provide detailed guidelines before your trip.

**You don't have to see everything.** The temptation is to cram every animal into your game drives. The reality is that 4 to 5 sightings a day is a great day. Trying to "complete the list" leads to exhaustion and frustration. Trust your guide. They know the animals, the patterns, and the timing.

## What to bring

A short packing list for a 7-day safari:

- Neutral-coloured clothing (khaki, olive, beige - no white, no bright colours)
- A fleece or warm jumper for early-morning drives
- Comfortable walking shoes
- Sandals for the lodge
- Sun hat and sunglasses
- High-SPF sunscreen
- DEET-based insect repellent
- Binoculars (essential)
- Camera with extra batteries and memory cards
- A book or e-reader for the downtime
- A reusable water bottle (your lodge will fill it)
- A small torch or headlamp

## The bottom line

A Tanzania safari is one of the most extraordinary experiences in travel. The days are slow, the landscapes are vast, and the animals are genuinely wild. You will be tired, dusty, and at some point slightly sunburnt. You will also see things you have never seen before, and you will come back changed.
