Ruaha is what Tanzania was like before mass tourism — and it remains so, with fewer than 1% of the country's visitors making it this far south. At over 20,000 km², Ruaha is larger than any of Tanzania's Northern Circuit parks combined. It feels vast, untouched, and remote, and the wildlife density in the dry season is genuinely impressive.

The park's most famous statistic is its lion population: around 10% of the world's remaining wild lions live in Ruaha, an extraordinary concentration in an ecosystem that receives a fraction of the attention given to the Serengeti. Lions are not just common here — they're easy to find. The pride sizes in Ruaha are notable, and the open miombo woodland makes sightings almost certain. Ruaha is also one of the most important strongholds for African wild dogs in East Africa, with multiple breeding packs regularly seen in the central areas.

What makes Ruaha especially interesting to naturalists is the mix of east and southern African species that overlaps here. Sable antelope — usually associated with the miombo woodlands of Zambia and Zimbabwe — are common. Greater kudu are also found in good numbers, as are the southern ground hornbill and several other miombo specialists that don't occur in the Northern Circuit. The combination of typical safari species (elephant, buffalo, hippo, crocodile) with these southern and woodland specialists gives Ruaha a unique character.

The park is best combined with a flight to the Iringa or Msembe airstrip. Most visitors spend 3–4 nights — two for game drives, one for a walking safari (allowed in Ruaha), and one for a night drive on a partner concession. The park is also an increasingly popular fly-in addition to a Northern Circuit safari for travellers who have the time.
