Katavi is the park for travellers who want a safari like it was 50 years ago. It receives fewer than 1,000 visitors a year, and you can drive for an entire morning without seeing another vehicle. The park is in the far west of Tanzania, in the catchment of Lake Tanganyika, and its remoteness is part of its appeal.

The defining spectacle of Katavi is the dry-season hippo concentration. The Katuma River shrinks to a series of muddy pools, and the park's hippos — estimated at 4,000 individuals — pile into them. It's not unusual to see 200 hippos in a single pool, fighting for space, with crocodiles lying in the middle of them. Predators know that the dry season is when the easiest meals are available: lions patrol the edges of the pools, and the crocs take young hippos. The drama is intense and entirely natural.

Away from the river, Katavi's floodplains fill with buffalo. Herds of 1,000+ are common, and they share the open ground with sable antelope, roan antelope, and big herds of elephant. The park is also a stronghold for roan antelope — one of the rarest large antelopes in East Africa — which require undisturbed miombo woodland and tend to disappear wherever there's too much human pressure.

Most visitors stay 3–4 nights in one of a handful of small, remote tented camps. Activities include game drives, walking safaris (one of the few parks that allows them), and night drives. Katavi is usually combined with Mahale Mountains National Park — the chimpanzee trekking destination on the shore of Lake Tanganyika — for a 7–10 day fly-in adventure that sees almost no other tourists.
