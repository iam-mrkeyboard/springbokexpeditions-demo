Mkomazi is the park for travellers who want to escape the safari crowds. Sitting along the northern border, Mkomazi covers 3,200 km² of dry, semi-arid bush that feels distinct from the rest of northern Tanzania. It is one of the least-visited parks in the country, which means you can have entire valleys to yourself — and a real chance of seeing species that are rare everywhere else in the region.

The park's most celebrated residents are the African wild dogs. Once locally extinct in the area, a reintroduction programme with the George Adamson Wildlife Preservation Trust and Tanzania National Parks has rebuilt a population that now numbers around 100 individuals. Wild dog tracking in Mkomazi is led by expert rangers and is one of the most exciting activities in East Africa — there's nothing quite like following a pack through the bush on foot.

The other headline conservation story is the black rhino sanctuary. A 45 km² enclosure in the south of the park has been used to breed black rhinos for reintroduction to other protected areas. A guided visit to the sanctuary is a rare opportunity to learn about the intensive work that goes into saving a critically endangered species.

Mkomazi is also a stronghold for the dry-country antelopes that have disappeared from most of East Africa: gerenuk (the long-necked "giraffe gazelle"), lesser kudu, and beisa oryx are all regularly seen. Combine a 2–3 day Mkomazi visit with the Usambara Mountains (for hiking and historical German colonial architecture) for a completely different take on northern Tanzania.
