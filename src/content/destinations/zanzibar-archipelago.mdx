Zanzibar is what most people picture when they imagine an Indian Ocean island. The archipelago is made up of two main islands - Unguja (usually just called Zanzibar) and Pemba - plus a handful of smaller islets. The main island is small (around 85 km by 30 km) but offers an extraordinary range of experiences: a UNESCO World Heritage city, working spice plantations, world-class beaches, and some of the best snorkelling and diving in East Africa.

The cultural heart is **Stone Town**, the old Arab–Swahili trading city on the western side of the island. Its narrow alleys are lined with carved wooden doors, mosques, merchants' houses, and the old slave market site - a moving place, and a reminder of the darker side of the trade that built the town. The Forodhani Gardens waterfront food market is the best place in East Africa to eat street food: sugar cane juice, seafood skewers, Zanzibar pizza, and the local urojo soup.

Away from Stone Town, the beaches on the north and east coasts are the main draw. **Nungwi** and **Kendwa** in the north have soft white sand and reliable swimming (most other beaches on the island are affected by tides). **Paje** on the east coast is the kite-surfing capital. The southeast coast has the more boutique, romantic properties. Offshore, **Mnemba Atoll** is a marine park with some of the best snorkelling in East Africa - clear water, healthy coral, and regular turtle and dolphin encounters.

Most visitors spend 3–5 nights on Zanzibar, either at the start of a Tanzania trip (for the beach) or at the end (after a safari). The most common itinerary is to combine 5–7 days on safari in the Northern Circuit with 4–5 days on the island.
