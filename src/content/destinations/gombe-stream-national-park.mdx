Gombe Stream is a tiny park — just 52 km², a narrow strip of forest and beach on the eastern shore of Lake Tanganyika. It is the smallest of Tanzania's national parks, but the most famous chimpanzee research site in the world. This is where Jane Goodall arrived in 1960 to study wild chimps, and where the research that transformed our understanding of primates — including the discovery that chimps make and use tools — was carried out.

The chimps of Gombe are the main reason to come. The Kasakela community has been studied continuously for over 60 years, and the chimps are habituated to human presence. Treks typically reach a chimp party within an hour or two of walking from the research station, and the experience of sitting a few metres from a wild chimp — watching them groom, feed, interact, and sometimes fight — is intimate and moving in a way that's hard to describe.

Gombe feels more like a research camp than a national park. There's no lodge, no restaurant, no pool deck with a view. The accommodation is simple tents or bandas near the research station, the food is communal, and the rangers are mostly former researchers and trackers. It's a different experience from the polished luxury of the Serengeti, and it attracts a different kind of traveller: people who care about conservation, who want to engage with what Goodall built, and who don't mind basic accommodation in exchange for a genuinely meaningful visit.

Most visitors stay 2–3 nights and combine with 4–5 nights in Mahale, the larger and more luxurious chimpanzee park further down the lake. The two together — Gombe for the history, Mahale for the wilderness — make for a powerful Western Circuit chimp itinerary.
