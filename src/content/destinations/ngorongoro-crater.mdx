The Ngorongoro Crater is often called the eighth natural wonder of the world, and once you've stood on the rim and looked down 600 metres into the world's largest intact volcanic caldera, the description feels accurate. The crater is roughly 19 km across, 610 m deep, and covers 260 km² - a self-contained ecosystem where the grass is always green, water is always available, and prey animals are reluctant to leave. Predators, by contrast, thrive.

Around 25,000 large animals live on the crater floor: lion, spotted hyena (numerically dominant here), elephant, buffalo, wildebeest, zebra, and - most prized - the critically endangered black rhinoceros. Around 30 individuals remain in the crater, making this one of the most reliable places in East Africa to see them in the wild. The crater also hosts a separate, non-migratory population of wildebeest and zebra, meaning you can plan a visit outside the Great Migration season and still see abundant plains game.

The other residents of the crater are the Maasai. The Ngorongoro Conservation Area is one of the few places in Tanzania where the Maasai live alongside wildlife - the conservation area was created in 1959 specifically to allow both Maasai pastoralism and wildlife conservation to continue. Community-led village visits on the rim give visitors a respectful, informative introduction to Maasai culture and the day-to-day realities of herding cattle in lion country.

Most safaris spend one night on the crater rim and descend for a half-day game drive the following morning, before continuing on to Lake Manyara, Tarangire, or the Serengeti. Combine the crater with Olduvai Gorge - a 45-minute drive from the rim - for a half-day detour to the museum at the site where Louis and Mary Leakey discovered some of the most important early hominid fossils ever found.
