Mahale is a long way from anywhere, and that's the point. The park is on the eastern shore of Lake Tanganyika, in the far west of Tanzania, and to get there you fly to Kigoma, then a small plane to a sand airstrip on the beach, then a boat to your camp. There's no road access. Visitors who make the journey tend to remember it as the highlight of their Tanzania trip.

The chimpanzees are the headline. Around 1,000 chimps live in the Mahale Mountains, and the M-troop — a community of about 60 individuals — has been studied continuously by Kyoto University researchers since 1965. The chimps are habituated, and treks typically get you within metres of them. Watching a chimp forage, groom, and play in a tropical forest is one of the great wildlife experiences, and Mahale is the only park in Tanzania where you can do it.

What makes Mahale different from other chimpanzee destinations is the setting. The forest rises directly from the beach, and after a morning trek you can snorkel in Lake Tanganyika — the world's second-deepest lake, with visibility sometimes over 30 metres. The lake is also home to over 250 species of cichlid, and the snorkelling is genuinely spectacular. Add beach time, kayaking, fishing, and a forest waterfall walk, and you have a multi-day experience that has very little in common with a typical safari.

Most visitors stay 4–5 nights, usually combined with 2–3 nights in Katavi National Park, reached by a short flight along the Katuma River. The combination is increasingly popular with second-time safari travellers who want a Tanzania trip that goes beyond the usual Northern Circuit.
