description: |
  Tanzania has one of the richest bird lists in Africa — over 1,100 recorded species, including 24 endemics and a string of rare and range-restricted birds. A good birding trip covers the country's distinct habitats: the highland forests around Arusha (where the endemic green-breasted pitta and the African black-headed oriole can be found), the soda lakes of the Rift Valley, the groundwater forests of Lake Manyara, the miombo woodlands of Tarangire, and the open plains of the Serengeti.

  We pair you with a specialist birding guide who knows the calls, knows the timings, and knows which local patches hold which endemics. The game drives are built around the birds first, and the larger mammals second. We can also arrange night walks for nightjars and owlets, and boat trips on Lake Manyara for waterbirds and the African fish eagle.

    title: Arusha National Park
    body: Full day in the highland forests. Targets include the endemic green-breasted pitta, African black-headed oriole, brown-breasted barbet, and the Hartlaub's turaco.
    location: Arusha
  - day: 3
    title: Arusha to Tarangire
    body: Drive to Tarangire. Afternoon drive in the miombo woodlands — yellow-collared lovebird, Ashy starling, the endemic Tanzanian red-billed hornbill.
    location: Tarangire
  - day: 4
    title: Tarangire
    body: Full day in Tarangire. The baobab-studded landscape is good for raptors — the bateleur, the martial eagle, the brown snake eagle. Pools hold African jacana and white-faced whistling duck.
    location: Tarangire
  - day: 5
    title: Tarangire to Lake Manyara
    body: Short drive to Lake Manyara, afternoon walk in the groundwater forest. Targets include the iconic lilac-breasted roller, the grey-headed kingfisher, and the African pygmy kingfisher.
    location: Lake Manyara
  - day: 6
    title: Lake Manyara
    body: Morning boat trip on the lake — huge flocks of pelicans, cormorants, and the African fish eagle. Afternoon drive to look for the Manyara endemics.

    title: Manyara to the Serengeti

faqs:
  - q: When is the best time for a birding safari?
    a: November to April is the wet season, which is best for migrants from Europe and the Palaearctic. The dry season (June to October) is also good, and the resident birds are easier to find.
  - q: Can we add a night walk?
    a: Yes, in the Lake Manyara and Tarangire areas. Night walks are excellent for nightjars, scops owls, and the Verreaux's eagle-owl.
  - q: How many species can we realistically see?
    a: A well-planned 12-day birding trip will yield 400–500 species, including most of the East African endemics.
  - q: Do I need to be a serious birder?
    a: No. The trip is built around birds, but the larger mammals are still very much part of the experience. Many of our birding clients come back raving about the lion sightings.
