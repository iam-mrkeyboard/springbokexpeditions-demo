description: |
  Tanzania's cultural landscape is as varied as its wildlife. The Maasai of the northern plains are the most visible, but the country is also home to the Hadzabe — one of the last remaining hunter-gatherer tribes on Earth, who have lived around Lake Eyasi for over 50,000 years — and the Datoga, traditional pastoralists and blacksmiths who have worked with iron for at least 2,000 years. On the coast, Stone Town is one of the oldest Swahili trading ports in East Africa, with a history that goes back to the Omani and the Persian traders of the 12th century.

  This trip is designed to spend real time with each community, not just drive through. The Hadzabe visit is a full day, with a morning hunt and a traditional meal in the bush. The Maasai visit is two nights in a community-run boma, with morning and evening activities. Stone Town gets a day and a half with a local guide.

faqs:
  - q: Are the community visits ethical?
    a: We work with a small set of communities that have asked for tourism, set their own fees, and control their own programmes. None of the visits are staged. The community fees we pay are split across the village.
  - q: Is the Hadzabe hunt real?
    a: Yes. The men still hunt small game with bows and poisoned arrows. They'll take you out for a morning hunt, and you'll see how it works. We do not guarantee that any animals will be caught, and the trip is built around the experience, not the kill.
  - q: Can I extend to a longer community stay?
    a: Yes. We can arrange week-long stays in any of the communities we work with — usually as part of a longer volunteer programme.
  - q: Is this suitable for children?
    a: Yes, from age 8 up. The Hadzabe visit in particular is something children tend to remember for the rest of their lives.
